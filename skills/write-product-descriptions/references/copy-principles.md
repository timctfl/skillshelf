# PDP Copy Principles

This reference file defines what "better" means when writing product descriptions from spec data. It is organized into two tiers: table stakes (the copy must clear these or it has failed) and what makes it good (these separate strong copy from adequate copy).

Read this file before writing. Use it as a self-check after drafting.

---

## Table Stakes

These are pass/fail. If the copy fails on any of these, the quality of everything else is irrelevant.

### 1. Follows the brand voice

The copy should sound like it was written by the brand's team, not by an AI or a generic copywriter. This means matching the brand's tone, sentence structure, vocabulary, and personality.

When a brand voice profile is available, follow it -- especially the avoidance rules and style decisions, which are the most concrete constraints. When no voice profile is available, look for cues in whatever the user has shared (their content template, how they write, existing copy on their site if referenced).

Common failures:
- Introducing superlatives or hype the brand doesn't use ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "ultimate")
- Shifting register (the brand writes casually but the copy sounds formal, or vice versa)
- Using filler phrases that sound like AI output ("whether you're a... or a...," "designed to help you," "take your X to the next level")
- Defaulting to a generic "ecommerce copywriter" voice instead of the brand's actual voice

### 2. Aware of the brand positioning

The copy should frame the product through the lens of what the brand stands for, not through generic category language. Two skincare brands might sell similar serums, but one positions around clinical efficacy and the other around clean ingredients and ritual. The copy should reflect that difference.

When a positioning brief is available, use it to guide how you describe what the product does and why it matters. When no brief is available, infer positioning from whatever brand context is visible.

Common failures:
- Writing category-generic copy that could appear on any brand's site
- Emphasizing attributes the brand doesn't lead with (e.g., writing performance-first copy for a brand that leads with sustainability)
- Contradicting the brand's positioning by introducing framing the brand wouldn't use

### 3. Follows the structure of the page

The copy must fit exactly into the brand's prescribed template sections. Same section names, same format (bullets vs. paragraphs vs. stat blocks), same approximate length, same constraints.

PDP templates exist because the ecommerce team needs to paste copy into a CMS with defined content slots. If the copy doesn't fit the slots, it doesn't ship.

Common failures:
- Writing a paragraph where the template expects bullets
- Ignoring visible character or word count constraints
- Merging or splitting sections the template keeps separate
- Renaming sections to generic labels ("Key Benefits" when the brand calls it "Why you'll love it")

### 4. Doesn't make things up

Every claim in the copy must trace back to the spec data the user provided. If you can't source a claim, don't include it.

This is the most critical principle when writing from specs because the temptation is strong. Spec sheets are often dry and sparse. The gap between "what the spec says" and "what would sound good on the PDP" is where fabrication happens.

This is especially important for:
- Ingredient claims and mechanisms of action
- Performance stats and test results
- Sourcing, certifications, and regulatory statements
- Specific outcomes ("reduces fine lines in 4 weeks") -- only include if the data supports it
- Benefits that go beyond what the spec data actually implies

When the spec data is thin, write what you can support and flag the gaps. Confident-sounding copy built on guesses is worse than shorter copy built on facts.

Common failures:
- Inventing benefits not implied by the spec data ("keeps you cool in summer heat" when the spec only mentions waterproofing)
- Upgrading vague specs to specific claims ("water-resistant" becoming "waterproof in sustained rain" without test data)
- Adding sensory details not in the data ("buttery-soft fabric," "whisper-quiet zipper")
- Filling thin sections with plausible-sounding but unsourced claims

## What Makes It Good

These are the difference between copy that clears the bar and copy that's worth using. They won't save bad copy, but they're what make good copy noticeably better.

### 5. Extracts specificity from the specs

Spec sheets hand you specificity on a plate. The main failure mode is ignoring it in favor of generic filler. Strong PDP copy uses the actual data: the specific material, the specific weight, the specific rating, the specific construction detail.

The skill's primary value-add is translating technical specificity into customer-relevant language without losing the specificity itself. "100% recycled nylon ripstop (2.5L laminate)" becomes context about what that means for the customer, but the specific material and construction stay in the copy. "330 g" stays as "330 g," not "lightweight."

Where to push for specificity:
- Hero descriptions: What does this product actually do, grounded in its actual specs? Not "keeps you dry" but what specifically about its construction keeps you dry.
- Features sections: Lead with the standout specs, not with generic benefit language.
- Materials sections: Preserve the full spec detail. Customers who scroll to materials want precision.

Where specificity matters less:
- Regulatory boilerplate
- Standard care instructions that are genuinely universal
- Section headers and navigation labels

### 6. Decision-driving details are easy to find

The job of PDP copy is to help the shopper decide whether this product is right for them. The information that drives that decision should be easy to find, not buried.

What "drives the decision" depends on the product. For a rain jacket, it might be waterproof rating and weight. For a serum, it might be the active ingredient and skin concern. For a backpack, it might be capacity and frame type. The skill should identify the decision-driving specs for this specific product and make sure they're prominent.

This doesn't mean every section leads with specs. Different sections have different jobs:
- A hero description might lead with the use case the product fits
- A features section might lead with the standout technical attribute
- A materials section should lead with the primary material

The principle is consistent across sections: put the most useful information where the shopper encounters it first within each section. Don't bury the standout spec behind preamble.

---

## Using These Principles

These six principles are ordered by priority. When they conflict, higher-numbered principles yield to lower-numbered ones.

For example: if making the copy more specific (principle 5) would require inventing a claim not in the spec data, principle 4 (don't make things up) wins. If restructuring a section to surface decision-driving details (principle 6) would break the template format, principle 3 (follow the structure) wins.

The table stakes (1-4) are non-negotiable. Principles 5 and 6 are where the skill adds the most value, but only within the constraints the table stakes set.
