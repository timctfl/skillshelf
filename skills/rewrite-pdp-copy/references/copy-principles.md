# PDP Copy Principles

This reference file defines what "better" means when rewriting PDP copy. It is organized into two tiers: table stakes (the copy must clear these or it has failed) and what makes it good (these separate strong copy from adequate copy).

Read this file before rewriting. Use it as a self-check after drafting.

---

## Table Stakes

These are pass/fail. If the rewritten copy fails on any of these, the quality of everything else is irrelevant.

### 1. Follows the brand voice

The copy should sound like it was written by the brand's team, not by an AI or a generic copywriter. This means matching the brand's tone, sentence structure, vocabulary, and personality.

When a brand voice profile is available, follow it -- especially the avoidance rules and style decisions, which are the most concrete constraints. When no voice profile is available, study the existing PDP copy and match its patterns.

Common failures:
- Introducing superlatives or hype the brand doesn't use ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "ultimate")
- Shifting register (the brand writes casually but the rewrite sounds formal, or vice versa)
- Using filler phrases that sound like AI output ("whether you're a... or a...," "designed to help you," "take your X to the next level")
- Smoothing out personality or quirks that make the brand's voice distinctive

### 2. Aware of the brand positioning

The copy should frame the product through the lens of what the brand stands for, not through generic category language. Two skincare brands might sell similar serums, but one positions around clinical efficacy and the other around clean ingredients and ritual. The copy should reflect that difference.

When a positioning brief is available, use it to guide how you describe what the product does and why it matters. When no brief is available, infer positioning from whatever brand context is visible (the template itself often reveals a lot about how the brand thinks about its products).

Common failures:
- Writing category-generic copy that could appear on any brand's site
- Emphasizing attributes the brand doesn't lead with (e.g., writing performance-first copy for a brand that leads with sustainability)
- Contradicting the brand's positioning by introducing framing the brand wouldn't use

### 3. Follows the structure of the page

The rewritten copy must fit exactly into the brand's prescribed template sections. Same section names, same format (bullets vs. paragraphs vs. stat blocks), same approximate length, same constraints.

This is not about being rigid for rigidity's sake. PDP templates exist because the ecomm team needs to paste copy into a CMS with defined content slots. If the rewrite doesn't fit the slots, it doesn't ship.

Common failures:
- Writing a paragraph where the template expects bullets
- Ignoring visible character or word count constraints
- Merging or splitting sections the template keeps separate
- Renaming sections to generic labels ("Key Benefits" when the brand calls it "Why you'll love it")

### 4. Doesn't make things up

Every claim in the copy must trace back to the product data, existing PDP copy, or review insights the user provided. If you can't source a claim, don't include it.

This is especially important for:
- Ingredient claims and mechanisms of action
- Efficacy stats and clinical results (carry through unchanged)
- Sourcing, certifications, and regulatory statements
- Customer quotes or attributed language
- Specific outcomes ("reduces fine lines in 4 weeks") -- only include if the data supports it

When the product data is thin, write what you can support and flag the gaps. Confident-sounding copy built on guesses is worse than shorter copy built on facts.

Common failures:
- Inventing ingredient benefits or mechanisms not in the product data
- Upgrading vague claims to specific ones without evidence ("helps with hydration" becoming "boosts hydration by 40%")
- Adding customer-language phrasing that didn't come from actual reviews
- Filling thin sections with plausible-sounding but unsourced claims

## What Makes It Good

These are the difference between copy that clears the bar and copy that's worth using. They won't save bad copy, but they're what make good copy noticeably better.

### 5. Specific to this product

Strong PDP copy says things that are true of this product and not easily interchangeable with a competitor in the same category. This comes from working the product data hard -- pulling out specific ingredients, mechanisms, use cases, materials, sourcing details, or customer outcomes rather than defaulting to category-level language.

Not every sentence needs to be unique to the product. Some things are category-standard and fine to state plainly ("apply to clean skin"). But the copy overall should leave no doubt about what makes this product this product.

Where to push for specificity:
- Hero descriptions: What does this product actually do, specifically? Not "nourishes your skin" but the specific mechanism or ingredient story.
- Benefits sections: What outcomes can the customer expect, and why? Tie benefits to specific product attributes when possible.
- Ingredient callouts: What does this ingredient do in this formulation, not just in general?
- Usage instructions: What's the specific context, routine, or scenario where this product fits?

Where specificity matters less:
- Regulatory boilerplate
- Standard usage instructions that are genuinely universal ("shake well before use")
- Section headers and navigation labels

When review insights are available, they're one of the best sources of specificity. Customers describe products in concrete terms that marketing copy often abstracts away. Use their language to inform (not replace) the copy.

### 6. Decision-driving details are easy to find

The job of PDP copy is to help the shopper decide whether this product is right for them. The information that drives that decision should be easy to find, not buried.

What "drives the decision" depends on the product and the section. For a $60 serum, it might be what skin concern it addresses and how it works. For a hiking boot, it might be waterproof rating and intended terrain. For a coffee subscription, it might be roast profile and origin. The skill should identify the decision-driving details for this specific product and make sure they're prominent.

This doesn't mean every section leads with specs. Different sections have different jobs:
- A hero description might lead with the core benefit or use case
- A benefits section might lead with outcomes
- A usage section might lead with the scenario or routine it fits into
- An FAQ might lead with the most common concern

The principle is consistent across sections: put the most useful information where the shopper encounters it first within each section. Don't make them read through preamble to find what they need.

Research supports this. Baymard Institute found that structuring descriptions with a scannable highlights layer before deeper detail increases engagement, because shoppers can quickly validate decision-critical attributes. (Baymard Institute, "Structuring Product Page Descriptions by 'Highlights' Increases User Engagement (Yet 78% of Sites Don't)," 2018). Nielsen Norman Group found that descriptions should answer real customer questions rather than lead with marketing fluff, since users skim and concentrate on the first lines. (Nielsen Norman Group, "UX Guidelines for Ecommerce Product Pages," 2019).

## Using These Principles

These six principles are ordered by priority. When they conflict, higher-numbered principles yield to lower-numbered ones.

For example: if making the copy more specific (principle 5) would require inventing a claim not in the product data, principle 4 (don't make things up) wins. If restructuring a section to surface decision-driving details (principle 6) would break the template format, principle 3 (follow the structure) wins.

The table stakes (1-4) are non-negotiable. Principles 5 and 6 are where the skill adds the most value, but only within the constraints the table stakes set.
